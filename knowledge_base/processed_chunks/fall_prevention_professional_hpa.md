Based on the professional Taiwan HPA *Elder Fall Prevention Work Handbook*, here is the extracted guidance for caregivers. This content focuses on practical observations and environmental adjustments to support the elder’s safety at home.

## Home Environmental Safety Assessment Checklist

Caregivers should regularly review the home environment using these criteria to identify potential risks:

*   **Floor and Walking Paths:**
    *   Are all walking paths clear of clutter, toys, or loose objects that could be tripped over?
    *   Are throw rugs removed or secured firmly with double-sided tape or non-slip backing?
    *   Are electrical cords, phone lines, and extension cords tucked away against walls and out of the walking path?
    *   Is the floor surface even, with no raised thresholds or loose floorboards?
*   **Lighting and Visibility:**
    *   Is the lighting in all rooms, hallways, and stairs bright enough to see clearly during both the day and night?
    *   Are light switches easily accessible near the entrance of every room so the elder doesn't have to walk in the dark?
    *   Are nightlights installed in the bedroom, hallway, and bathroom to guide the elder during nighttime movement?
*   **Bathroom Safety:**
    *   Are non-slip mats or anti-slip strips placed inside the shower and on the bathroom floor?
    *   Are sturdy grab bars installed near the toilet and inside the shower area to provide support?
    *   Is the floor kept dry at all times, with spills cleaned up immediately?
*   **Bedroom and Living Areas:**
    *   Is the bed height appropriate (approximately 45-50 cm), allowing the elder’s feet to touch the floor comfortably when sitting on the edge?
    *   Is there enough space around the bed and in the living room for the elder to move with a cane or walker if needed?
    *   Are frequently used items kept within easy reach to avoid the need for stools or excessive stretching?

## Mobility Observations for Caregivers

Caregivers can observe the following behavioral patterns to understand the elder's physical confidence and balance:

*   **Rising from a Seated Position:** Observe how the elder stands up from a chair. If the elder seems to struggle or needs to use their arms significantly for support, it may indicate a change in lower limb strength. A specific observation is the "30-second chair stand"—noting how comfortably the elder can repeat this movement.
*   **Walking Pace and Confidence:** Note the time it takes for the elder to rise from a chair, walk 3 meters, turn around, walk back, and sit down. If this sequence takes longer than 12 seconds, the elder might benefit from additional environmental support or a more focused activity routine.
*   **Balance and Stability:** Watch the elder while they stand on one leg (with a nearby support for safety). A behavioral pattern where the elder can only maintain balance for a few seconds suggests that balance-focused activities, such as Tai Chi, might be beneficial.
*   **Stair Navigation:** Pay attention to whether the elder hesitates or grips the handrails tightly when using the stairs. This observation can help determine if the lighting or stair visibility needs improvement.

## Priority Home Modification Items

To create a safer living space, prioritize these environmental adjustments based on the frequency of use and level of risk:

*   **Eliminate Tripping Hazards:** Remove all door thresholds or install small ramps to create a seamless floor surface. Ensure all wires are permanently secured away from walking paths.
*   **Install Support Bars:** Place high-quality grab bars in high-risk areas like the bathroom, along long hallways, and on both sides of any staircases.
*   **Apply Anti-Slip Surfaces:** Use professional-grade anti-slip coatings or mats in the bathroom. Apply high-contrast anti-slip strips to the edges of all steps to improve visual depth perception.
*   **Optimize Furniture Height:** Adjust the height of chairs and beds so that when the elder sits, their knees are at a 90-degree angle and their feet are flat on the floor.
*   **Maintenance of Mobility Aids:** Regularly check the rubber tips of canes and walkers for wear. Ensure they provide adequate grip on various floor surfaces.

## Caregiver Guidance for Daily Support

*   **Supportive Physical Activity:** Encourage the elder to engage in regular activities that focus on lower limb strength and balance. Gentle movements like sitting and standing or practicing balance while holding a sturdy surface can be integrated into the daily routine.
*   **The Three-Step Rising Routine:** Guide the elder to follow a slow rising sequence to allow their body to adjust. When waking up, they should lie still for 1 minute, sit at the bedside for 1 minute, and stand steadily for 1 minute before walking.
*   **Reviewing Daily Factors:** Regularly observe if the elder seems unusually tired or dizzy compared to their usual pattern. If such patterns are noticed, it may be helpful to review their daily routines and any health factors with a professional.
*   **Footwear Selection:** Help the elder choose shoes that fit snugly, have thin but non-slip soles, and use velcro or laces for a secure fit. Avoid loose-fitting slippers or walking in socks on smooth floors.
*   **Clear Communication:** When assisting the elder with movement, provide clear, step-by-step guidance and allow ample time for them to respond. If a behavioral pattern change is observed, accompanying the elder to a professional would be helpful.
