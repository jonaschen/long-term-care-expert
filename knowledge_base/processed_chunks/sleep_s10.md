## Cognitive Approaches to Better Sleep

Sometimes, the way an elder thinks about sleep can affect how well they actually rest. It is common for those experiencing sleep changes to feel anxious or frustrated when they cannot fall asleep quickly. This anxiety can create a cycle where the worry about not sleeping makes it even harder to relax. Encouraging a more flexible perspective on sleep can be very helpful.

For instance, if the elder cannot fall asleep after 20 minutes, it is often better for them to get out of bed and do a quiet, low-light activity in another room rather than lying in bed feeling tense. They can return to bed when they feel sleepy again. This helps the brain maintain a strong association between the bed and sleep, rather than between the bed and frustration. Families can also help the elder manage their expectations; for example, needing only 6 or 7 hours of sleep as an older adult is often perfectly normal. Avoiding a "fixed" idea of exactly when sleep must happen can reduce pressure. Encouraging the elder to focus on the resting and relaxation aspects of being in bed, rather than "forcing" sleep, often leads to a more peaceful night. By fostering a calm and positive attitude toward rest, families can help break the cycle of nighttime worry.
