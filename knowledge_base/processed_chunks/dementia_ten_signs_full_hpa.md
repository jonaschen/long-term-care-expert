# Dementia Ten Signs Full Hpa

Based on the Taiwan Health Promotion Administration guidelines, here are ten behavioral observations for families to keep in mind, reframed to focus on supportive home care:

1. **Changes in Recent Memory**
   The elder seems to forget recently shared information or events more frequently than before. For instance, you might notice them buying the same groceries multiple times in one day or forgetting a visitor’s name even after being reminded several times.

2. **Challenges with Familiar Routines**
   Daily tasks that used to be second nature may now seem difficult for them to complete. A concrete example is a seasoned home cook who suddenly finds it hard to follow the steps of a favorite recipe, resulting in a dish that tastes very different from their usual pattern.

3. **Difficulty with Expressive Language**
   You might notice the elder struggling to find common words or losing their train of thought during a conversation. For example, they might call a "watch" a "wrist-clock" or find it hard to follow a fast-paced family discussion they previously enjoyed.

4. **Time and Place Disorientation**
   The elder's sense of "where" and "when" may appear slightly blurred compared to their usual clarity. You might observe them feeling momentarily confused about how to get home from a nearby park or being unsure whether it is morning or evening.

5. **Changes in Practical Judgment**
   Decisions regarding safety or daily logic may seem out of character for the elder. For instance, they might choose to wear a thick sweater on a very hot afternoon or purchase food items that are clearly no longer fresh.

6. **Overwhelm with Complex Thinking**
   Tasks involving numbers or multi-step logic can become quite frustrating for them. A daily example would be the elder feeling completely overwhelmed when trying to manage a simple utility bill or balance a household budget they used to handle easily.

7. **Misplacing Items in Unusual Spots**
   Objects may begin to appear in very unexpected locations around the house. You might find their reading glasses in the refrigerator or a potted plant inside a kitchen cupboard, and they may be unable to retrace their steps to find what they lost.

8. **Shifts in Emotional Expression**
   The elder’s mood might seem more unpredictable or fluctuate more rapidly than it once did. For example, they may become suddenly tearful or upset without a clear external cause, even during a peaceful family gathering.

9. **Reduced Initiative in Hobbies**
   A noticeable change in how they spend their free time might occur, where they seem less interested in activities they once loved. You might observe them sitting quietly in front of the television for many hours, needing extra encouragement to join in social walks or gardening.

10. **Noticeable Personality Shifts**
    The elder’s general temperament may seem different, perhaps becoming more anxious, suspicious, or unusually dependent on a specific family member. For instance, a person who was once very outgoing might become very quiet and withdrawn in the presence of guests.

### What Families Can Do

*   **Create a Supportive Environment:** Use simple labels on drawers or keep frequently used items in consistent, visible places to help the elder navigate their home more easily.
*   **Encourage Gentle Engagement:** Gently invite the elder to participate in low-stress social activities, such as a short walk in a familiar park or listening to favorite music together.
*   **Establish a Relaxed Routine:** Keeping a consistent daily schedule for meals and rest can provide a sense of security and help the elder feel more grounded in their daily pattern.
*   **Seek Professional Guidance:** If these behavioral pattern changes continue or cause concern, accompanying the elder to a professional for a gentle conversation would be a helpful next step.
