## How Families Can Support Without Over-Assisting

Supporting an elder requires a careful balance between ensuring their safety and respecting their autonomy. While it can be tempting to take over tasks to prevent the elder from struggling, 'over-assisting' can accidentally lead to a decrease in the elder's physical capability and confidence. The goal is to be a supportive partner who encourages the elder to do as much as they can for themselves, providing a 'safety net' rather than taking the lead.

Families should focus on providing gentle encouragement and 'nudges' rather than direct instructions. For example, instead of saying 'you need to walk today,' you might say 'I'd love to take a short walk to see the flowers, would you like to come with me?' This approach respects the elder's choice while still promoting activity. Celebrate small successes—or 'daily wins'—such as the elder walking a bit further than yesterday or successfully handling a household task. These moments of recognition build the elder's confidence and motivate them to keep trying.

Observe the elder's response to your support. If they seem to be becoming more reliant on you for tasks they used to do alone, it may be a sign to step back slightly and look for ways to make the task easier for them to handle independently. Provide a steady hand or a watchful eye when needed, but allow the elder to maintain their own rhythm. By supporting without over-assisting, you help the elder preserve their sense of purpose and their ability to live a capable and independent life at home.
