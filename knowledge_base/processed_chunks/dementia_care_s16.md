## When to Seek Additional Support

Recognizing when you need more help is a sign of a responsible caregiver. You might consider seeking additional support if the elder's behavioral patterns change in ways that you can no longer manage safely at home, or if you feel your own health is beginning to suffer. For example, if nighttime restlessness becomes constant or if the elder's physical stability changes significantly, it is helpful to consult with a professional who can offer environmental and lifestyle guidance.

There are many avenues for support. You might start by calling the **1966 Long-term Care Hotline** to learn about home care assistance or respite services. For immediate emotional support or practical advice on behavioral challenges, you can reach out to specialized caregiver support lines. If you observe that the elder seems to be in physical pain but cannot express it, or if they are no longer able to eat or drink enough, these are clear signs that professional observation is needed. Early intervention and seeking help from community resources can prevent crises and ensure that both you and the elder receive the care you deserve. Remember, you do not have to do this alone; a whole network of professional support is available to assist you.
