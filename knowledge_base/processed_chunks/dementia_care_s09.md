## Outdoor Safety and Supervision

Spending time outdoors can be very beneficial for an elder's mood and physical health, but it requires careful planning to ensure safety. You might consider accompanying the elder on walks in familiar, quiet areas like a local park. Observe their surroundings—crowded or noisy streets can be overwhelming and increase the risk of confusion. It is helpful to visit outdoor spaces during quieter times of the day and to ensure the elder is wearing comfortable, non-slip footwear.

To manage the risk of the elder becoming separated from you, you might consider using an identification tag that lists contact information. Informing trusted people in your neighborhood about the elder's situation can create an extra layer of safety. If the elder enjoys gardening, you might consider creating a safe, enclosed outdoor space where they can work with familiar plants. Providing a clear and visible 'way back' to the house or a designated seating area can help them feel secure. If you observe the elder appearing hesitant or anxious in open spaces, stay close and provide gentle physical or verbal reassurance. By focusing on supervised and predictable outdoor activities, you allow the elder to enjoy the benefits of fresh air while minimizing the risks of getting lost or feeling overwhelmed.
