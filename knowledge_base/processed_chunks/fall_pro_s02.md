## Bathroom Safety and Hazard Checklist

The bathroom is often the highest-risk area in a home due to wet surfaces and the physical transitions required for daily hygiene. To improve safety, you might consider a thorough assessment of both the floor and the fixtures. Non-slip mats or professional anti-slip adhesive strips should be placed both inside the shower or bathtub and on the floor directly outside. It is helpful to ensure these mats have a strong grip and do not have edges that curl upward, as curled edges themselves can become a hazard.

Installing sturdy, high-quality grab bars is one of the most effective environmental adjustments you can make. These should be placed near the toilet and inside the shower area to provide reliable points of contact. You might consider a behavioral observation: if the elder frequently leans on the sink or a towel rack for support, it is a clear sign that a dedicated grab bar is needed in that location. 

Maintaining a dry environment is vital; any spills or condensation should be wiped up immediately. You might also consider the height of the toilet. If the elder seems to struggle when sitting or rising, a raised toilet seat or a sturdy toilet safety frame can provide the necessary leverage without requiring excessive physical effort. Good lighting is equally important here, as many falls occur during nighttime visits. Motion-sensor nightlights can provide immediate illumination without the elder needing to search for a switch in the dark.
