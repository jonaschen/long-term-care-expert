## Reducing Sedentary Time Throughout the Day

one of the most effective ways to support an elder's health is to follow the principle of "move more and sit less." Long periods of sitting, whether watching television or reading, can lead to physical stiffness and a decrease in overall energy. Reducing sedentary time does not always require a new exercise routine; it can be achieved by simply breaking up long periods of inactivity with short bursts of movement. These small breaks help keep the muscles engaged and support better circulation throughout the body.

Families can help by identifying 'sitting traps' in the elder's daily rhythm. If you notice the elder has been stationary for more than an hour, you might offer a gentle nudge. This could be as simple as suggesting a short walk to the window to look at the garden or asking for a little help with a light task in another room. Another strategy is to encourage movement during natural breaks in the day, such as standing up and stretching during television commercials or walking around the house while talking on the phone.

Every bit of movement counts toward a more active lifestyle. Even standing up for a few seconds every hour can make a difference in how the elder feels. You might notice that after a day with more frequent breaks from sitting, the elder seems more alert and moves with more ease. By making movement a frequent and natural part of the day, you help the elder maintain a behavioral pattern that supports their long-term physical function and daily comfort.
