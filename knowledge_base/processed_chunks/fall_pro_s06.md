## Staircase Safety and Visual Cues

Staircases represent a significant environmental challenge, but specific modifications can greatly improve their safety. You might consider ensuring that sturdy handrails are installed on both sides of every staircase, extending slightly beyond the top and bottom steps to provide continuous support. It is essential that these handrails are professionally secured and can support the elder's full weight if they need to lean on them. 

Visibility is a key factor in stair safety. Bright lighting should be available at both the top and bottom of the stairs to eliminate shadows that can hide the edges of steps. You might consider applying high-contrast, anti-slip strips to the edge of each step. This visual cue helps the elder clearly distinguish where one step ends and the next begins, which is especially important as depth perception patterns change. 

If you observe the elder hesitating at the top of the stairs or gripping the handrails more tightly than usual, it may be a sign that more activities should be prioritized on the ground floor to reduce the frequency of stair use. Ensure that stairs are never used as a temporary storage area for books, laundry, or other items. By maintaining clear, well-lit, and well-supported stairways, you provide a safer environment for those times when moving between levels is necessary. If the elder's movement on stairs seems significantly altered compared to their usual pattern, you might consider a professional review of their mobility.
