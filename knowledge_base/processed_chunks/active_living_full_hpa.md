## Recommended Weekly Activity Targets

The Taiwan Health Promotion Administration suggests that staying active is a fundamental part of daily life. For those seeking to maintain their physical function, the following targets are recommended:

*   **Total Volume:** Aim for at least **150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity** per week.
*   **Session Duration:** Activity should be performed in bouts of at least **10 minutes** at a time to count toward the weekly goal.
*   **Vigorous Option:** Alternatively, one might perform **20 minutes of vigorous activity** at least **3 times a week**.
*   **Variety is Key:** A balanced routine should include:
    *   **Strength:** Low weight with higher repetitions.
    *   **Balance:** Gradually reducing support to increase difficulty.
    *   **Stretching:** Holding positions for 10–30 seconds, 3–5 times per set.
    *   **Aerobic:** Activities that make you feel slightly breathless but still able to speak.

## Household Activities and Intensity Levels

Many daily chores count toward physical activity goals. The handbook categorizes them by the level of effort required:

*   **Light Intensity:** Self-care tasks such as bathing, dressing, and eating; light housework like washing dishes, wiping furniture, folding laundry, or watering flowers; and outings like shopping or taking the bus.
*   **Moderate Intensity:** Activities that cause a slight increase in breathing, such as mopping floors, vacuuming, hand-washing clothes, wiping windows, or cleaning the balcony. Carrying groceries (e.g., 5kg of rice or large water bottles), gardening, and walking to the store are also included.
*   **Vigorous Intensity:** Tasks that significantly increase heart rate and breathing, such as scrubbing floors on hands and knees, moving large furniture, carrying heavy items (11–22kg) up stairs, or heavy labor like shoveling and farming.

## Lifestyle-Integrated Exercises

The elder can incorporate specific movements into their daily routine to enhance strength and stability:

*   **The Big Reach:** When reaching for objects, sit slightly further away and extend the arm and body to lengthen the muscles. 
    *   *Benefit:* Improves flexibility and muscle length.
*   **Kitchen Lunges:** While cooking or waiting for water to boil, take a step to the side and shift the body weight.
    *   *Benefit:* Enhances lower body lateral strength and balance.
*   **Slow Sit-to-Stand:** When sitting down or getting up from a chair, do so as slowly as possible (aiming for 15–30 seconds).
    *   *Benefit:* Increases leg muscle power and knee stability.
*   **The Laundry Stretch:** Hang clothes on a high rod to encourage shoulder extension and reaching.
    *   *Benefit:* Maintains shoulder mobility and upper body strength.
    *   *Safety:* If the elder feels discomfort in the shoulder, they should avoid forcing the movement.
*   **Seated Leg Extensions:** While watching TV or chatting, lift one leg, straighten the knee, and hold for 5–20 seconds before switching.
    *   *Benefit:* Strengthens the thighs and stabilizes the knees.
*   **Pelvic Bridge:** Before sleeping, lie on the bed with knees bent and lift the hips at least 3cm off the mattress, holding for 15 seconds.
    *   *Benefit:* Supports core stability and pelvic floor function.

## Safety Tips for Daily Movement

To ensure the elder remains safe while being active, consider the following environmental and physical guidelines:

*   **Environment:** Ensure the space is well-lit, ventilated, and free of clutter or loose wires. Floors should be dry and non-slip.
*   **Equipment:** Use stable, non-wobbly chairs. When starting new balance exercises, the elder should always have a sturdy piece of furniture or a railing nearby to hold onto.
*   **Attire:** Wear well-fitting flat shoes; avoid exercising in just socks on slippery floors.
*   **Hydration & Breathing:** Drink water regularly. When performing strength moves, the elder should count out loud to avoid holding their breath.
*   **When to Stop:** Activities should cease immediately if the elder feels dizzy, has a headache, experiences chest tightness or palpitations, or feels cold sweats. If joint pain lasts more than 1–2 hours after activity, the intensity may have been too high.
