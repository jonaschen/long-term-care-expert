Based on Taiwan's National Physical Activity Guidelines (全民身體活動指引) and related Health Promotion Administration (HPA) resources, here is the extracted information regarding physical activity for older adults (65+), adapted to meet non-SaMD compliance standards.

## Recommended Activity Framework for Older Adults
The HPA provides a comprehensive framework for maintaining physical function through regular activity. For adults aged 65 and above, the primary goal is to integrate movement into daily life to support overall wellbeing and independence.

*   **Total Volume:** Aim for at least **150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity** per week. This can be achieved through various daily tasks and structured exercises.
*   **Session Consistency:** Activity should be performed in sessions of at least **10 minutes** at a time to effectively contribute to the weekly goal.
*   **Vigorous Option:** For those who maintain a high level of fitness, **20 minutes of vigorous activity** at least **3 times a week** is an alternative.
*   **A Balanced Routine:** A well-rounded approach includes:
    *   **Aerobic Activity:** Rhythmic movements that increase breathing slightly while still allowing the elder to speak comfortably.
    *   **Muscle Strengthening:** Utilizing low weights or body resistance with higher repetitions at least twice a week.
    *   **Balance Training:** Activities like Tai Chi or standing on one leg (with sturdy support) to enhance stability and coordination.
    *   **Flexibility:** Stretching movements held for 10–30 seconds to maintain the range of motion in joints.

## Understanding Intensity Levels with Practical Examples
Physical effort is categorized into three levels based on how the body responds. Families can use these examples to encourage the elder to stay active throughout the day.

*   **Light Intensity:** These tasks involve minimal effort and do not significantly change breathing or heart rate. Examples include:
    *   Self-care tasks such as bathing, dressing, and eating.
    *   Light household chores like washing dishes, wiping furniture, folding laundry, or watering flowers.
    *   Gentle outings such as shopping or taking public transportation.
*   **Moderate Intensity:** These activities cause a noticeable but comfortable increase in breathing. Examples include:
    *   Mopping floors, vacuuming, or hand-washing laundry.
    *   Carrying groceries (e.g., approximately 5kg) or gardening.
    *   Walking at a steady pace to a nearby store.
*   **Vigorous Intensity:** These tasks significantly increase heart rate and breathing. Examples include:
    *   Scrubbing floors or moving larger pieces of furniture.
    *   Carrying heavier items (up to 20kg) up a flight of stairs.
    *   Heavy labor like shoveling or active farming tasks.

## Supporting Activity for Limited Mobility
When an elder experiences changes in their usual mobility pattern, physical activity remains vital but should be adapted to their current capabilities.

*   **Seated Exercises:** Many strengthening movements can be performed while sitting. For instance, seated leg extensions (lifting one leg and holding for 5–20 seconds) help maintain thigh strength and knee stability.
*   **Integration into Routine:** Encourage the elder to reach for objects ("The Big Reach") or perform side steps ("Kitchen Lunges") while waiting for water to boil to improve lateral strength.
*   **Gradual Transitions:** The "Sit and Wait" method is essential. Encourage the elder to sit at the edge of the bed for a few moments before standing to allow their body to adjust.
*   **Slow Movements:** Practicing a "Slow Sit-to-Stand" (taking 15–30 seconds to rise from a chair) can significantly enhance lower body power and balance.

## Benefits of Regular Physical Activity
Engaging in consistent movement provides numerous lifestyle and functional benefits that support the elder’s quality of life.

*   **Functional Independence:** Regular activity helps the elder maintain the strength needed for daily self-care tasks.
*   **Improved Stability:** Balance-focused exercises reduce the likelihood of unexpected falls and increase confidence during movement.
*   **Muscle and Bone Support:** Weight-bearing and resistance activities help preserve muscle mass and support bone integrity.
*   **Enhanced Wellbeing:** Movement is often associated with better sleep patterns and a more positive daily mood.
*   **Cardiovascular Efficiency:** Aerobic activities support the heart and lungs, making daily tasks feel less tiring.

## Safety Precautions and Environmental Support
Ensuring a safe environment is just as important as the activity itself. Families should observe the elder’s behavioral patterns and adjust the surroundings accordingly.

*   **Environmental Lighting:** Ensure all pathways, especially to the bathroom, are well-lit with motion-sensor nightlights to prevent trips during the night.
*   **Proper Footwear:** The elder should wear flat-soled, non-slip shoes with secure fastenings like velcro. Avoid loose slippers or long trousers that might cause a stumble.
*   **Hydration and Pacing:** Encourage the elder to drink water regularly and take breaks. If they have been stationary for a long time, suggesting a brief stand-and-stretch every hour is helpful.
*   **Professional Guidance:** If you observe that a change in the elder's movement pattern persists over several days, you might consider accompanying them to a professional at a community health center for a general wellbeing review.
