## Lighting and Spatial Orientation

Proper lighting is essential for helping an elder perceive their environment accurately and move safely throughout the home. Changes in how the brain processes visual information can make shadows appear like holes or dark patches like obstacles. To prevent this, aim for even, bright lighting in all living areas. You might consider using high-wattage, non-glare bulbs and ensuring that entryways and stairs are especially well-lit to reduce the risk of trips.

Nighttime orientation is equally important. Placing motion-sensor nightlights in the hallway between the bedroom and the bathroom can help the elder find their way if they wake up during the night. Large, easy-to-read clocks and calendars in every room can also assist with time orientation, helping the elder distinguish between morning and evening. Use contrasting colors to define spaces; for instance, a brightly colored toilet seat can make the bathroom easier to navigate against white tiles, and a dark rug on a light floor can clearly mark a transition between rooms. If the elder seems hesitant to enter a room, observe if there are harsh glares from windows or deep shadows in corners. Adjusting curtains or adding floor lamps can create a more inviting and predictable space, helping the elder feel more secure as they move from one room to another.
