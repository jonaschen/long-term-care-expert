## Communication Approaches

Effective communication is built on patience, respect, and understanding the elder's current perspective. When you observe that the elder is having difficulty finding words or following a conversation, you might consider adjusting your approach. Speak clearly and at a slightly slower pace, using simple sentences. It is often more effective to ask one question at a time and give the elder plenty of time to respond without rushing them. Rushing can lead to frustration and may cause the elder to withdraw from the interaction.

Non-verbal cues are just as powerful as words. Maintaining gentle eye contact and a calm, friendly tone of voice conveys safety and affection. You might also use gestures or point to objects to clarify what you are saying. For example, when suggesting a snack, you could hold up a piece of fruit. If the elder becomes upset, try to focus on the feeling behind their words rather than correcting their facts. Validating their emotions—such as saying, 'I see that you are feeling worried'—can be much more comforting than an argument. Always treat the elder with the dignity they deserve, avoiding 'baby talk' or speaking about them as if they are not in the room. By staying calm and flexible, you can maintain a meaningful connection even when verbal communication becomes more challenging.
