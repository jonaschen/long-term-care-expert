## What Families Can Do Today

If you observe several of these patterns in your loved one, the most important step is to remain observant and supportive. Start by keeping a simple daily log of the behaviors you notice, including the time of day and what was happening in the environment. This record can be very helpful if you eventually decide to consult a professional for further guidance. Focus on creating a safe, comfortable, and low-stress home environment.

**Supporting the Home Environment:** Look for ways to reduce clutter and improve lighting, which can help reduce confusion and prevent trips or falls. Use high-contrast colors to help items stand out—for example, a brightly colored plate can make it easier for the elder to see their food. 

**Gentle Approach:** Remember that these changes are not the elder's fault. They are experiencing a change in their behavioral patterns. Your patience, love, and willingness to adapt the environment are the most valuable tools you have. If these observations continue or become more frequent, it is a good idea to seek out a professional consultation to discuss these patterns and learn more about supportive care strategies.
