## Responding to Wandering

Sometimes, an elder may seem to move about the house aimlessly or express a desire to 'go home' even when they are already there. This behavior, often called wandering, may be a sign that the elder is looking for something, is bored, or is feeling restless. To help keep the elder safe, you might consider several environmental strategies. Installing secure but easy-to-use locks on exit doors, or using 'hidden' locks placed high or low out of the elder's direct line of sight, can prevent them from leaving the house unaccompanied.

Technology and community support can provide additional peace of mind. You might consider having the elder wear a discreet identification bracelet or a GPS tracking device. It is also helpful to notify trusted neighbors and local shopkeepers so they can contact you if they see the elder out alone. Inside the home, ensure that the elder has plenty of safe space to walk. If they seem determined to go somewhere, sometimes walking with them for a short distance and then gently redirecting them back home can be more effective than trying to stop them abruptly. Observe if there are specific triggers for wandering, such as a noisy environment or a need to use the bathroom. By addressing these underlying needs and securing the environment, you can significantly reduce the risks associated with this behavior.
