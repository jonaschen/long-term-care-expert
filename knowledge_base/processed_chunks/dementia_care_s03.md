## Morning and Evening Routines

Establishing clear transitions for the start and end of the day can help the elder orient themselves to the passage of time. In the morning, you might consider opening the curtains to let in natural light, which helps signal that the day has begun. A calm, unhurried morning routine—perhaps involving gentle music and a familiar breakfast—can reduce anxiety as the elder transitions from sleep to activity. Observe if the elder seems more energetic in the early hours; this may be the best time for more complex tasks like dressing or light household engagement.

As the day winds down, an evening routine becomes essential for promoting relaxation. You might consider dimming the lights and reducing environmental noise an hour before the usual rest time. Engaging in a quiet 'wind-down' activity, such as looking at a book or listening to soft radio programs, can help the elder prepare for sleep. If you observe increased restlessness as the sun sets, providing extra reassurance and a familiar comfort item, like a soft blanket, can be helpful. Maintaining these consistent morning and evening 'anchors' helps regulate the elder's internal sense of timing and fosters a feeling of safety throughout the day and night.
