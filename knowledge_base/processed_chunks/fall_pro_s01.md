## Home Environmental Safety Overview

Creating a safe home environment for an elder begins with a systematic review of all living spaces to identify potential tripping hazards. Based on the Taiwan Health Promotion Administration guidelines, a primary focus should be on maintaining clear and unobstructed walking paths throughout the house. You might consider walking through each room from the elder's perspective, looking for items that could catch a foot or cause a slip. 

Common hazards include loose electrical cords, telephone lines, and extension cables that cross frequently used paths. These should be permanently routed along walls or secured under professional cable covers. Decorative items, such as low-lying furniture, baskets, or toys, should be moved to the periphery of the room to ensure wide, straight passages. Floors should be kept even, as even a small raised threshold between rooms can lead to a stumble. If thresholds cannot be removed, you might consider installing small, low-profile ramps to create a seamless transition. 

Compared to the usual pattern of leaving small items on the floor, adopting a 'zero-clutter' policy in walking areas is essential. Regularly check for loose floorboards or peeling linoleum that might require repair. By observing how the elder navigates these spaces, you can identify if they are subtly avoiding certain areas, which may indicate a hidden hazard that needs attention. Maintaining this level of environmental awareness is a foundational step in supporting the elder's continued independence and safety within their home.
