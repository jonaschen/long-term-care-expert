## Validating Emotional Needs

When communicating with an elder whose memory patterns are changing, focusing on their emotional experience is often more effective than focusing on factual accuracy. If the elder expresses ideas that seem factually incorrect or asks for things from the distant past, you might consider the feeling behind their words. For example, if they are looking for a childhood home, they may be expressing a need for comfort, safety, or belonging. Acknowledge these feelings by saying something like, 'I can see you are missing home; it's a place where you felt very safe.'

Avoiding arguments or 'reality testing' helps prevent unnecessary frustration and maintains the elder's dignity. Instead of correcting them, use the **Validation** strategy to join them in their reality. You might ask them to tell you more about the person or place they are thinking about, which allows them to share their memories and feel heard. If you observe the elder becoming upset because they cannot find something, acknowledge the frustration of losing a valued item rather than debating its existence. This approach prioritizes the relationship and the elder's emotional wellbeing, fostering a sense of connection and security even when their perception of the world differs from your own.
