## Supporting the Elder During Daily Transitions

Assisting an elder with movement requires a patient, step-by-step approach that respects their pace and promotes safety. For transitions like waking up and getting out of bed, you might consider guiding the elder through a 'three-step rising routine.' This involves lying still for one minute after waking, sitting at the bedside for one minute, and then standing steadily for one minute before taking the first step. This allows the body to adjust and helps prevent the dizziness that can sometimes occur when rising too quickly.

When providing physical support while walking, it is helpful to stand slightly to one side and slightly behind the elder, offering your arm for them to hold if they feel unsteady. This position allows you to provide stability without obstructing their path or their view of the floor. Always communicate your intentions clearly, such as saying, 'We are going to walk toward the kitchen now,' to help them feel prepared and in control.

If you observe the elder appearing frustrated or rushed, encourage them to take their time. Providing gentle verbal cues and allowing for frequent rest stops can make a significant difference in their confidence. Compared to a more hurried approach, this supportive and observant style of caregiving creates a calmer environment and reduces the likelihood of an accidental stumble during the many transitions of a typical day.
