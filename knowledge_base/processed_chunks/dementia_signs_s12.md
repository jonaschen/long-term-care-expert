## Supporting Without Creating Anxiety

When you notice changes in an elder’s behavior, it is natural to feel concerned or want to correct them. However, constant corrections can lead to the elder feeling anxious, defensive, or embarrassed. The goal of supportive care is to maintain the elder’s dignity and sense of self while ensuring their safety and comfort. This involves shifting from a focus on "facts" to a focus on "feelings."

**Validating the Experience:** If the elder is confused about the time or a person's name, try to go along with their reality whenever it is safe to do so. For example, if they think it is time to go to work, instead of saying "You've been retired for years," you might say, "You were always so hardworking. Let's have a cup of tea before we get started on anything today."

**Communication Tips:** Use short, simple sentences and speak clearly at a natural volume. Maintain eye contact and use gentle gestures to help convey your meaning. If the elder becomes frustrated, it is often better to pause, change the subject, or move to a different room to provide a fresh start. Your calm presence is more important than the specific words you use.
