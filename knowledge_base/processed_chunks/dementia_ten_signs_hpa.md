## Ten Behavioral Changes Worth Noticing Over Time

These are observable changes in day-to-day behavior and routines — not assessment criteria.
Families should look for *sustained changes from the elder's own usual pattern*, not
isolated incidents.

1. **Memory changes affecting daily life:** Frequently forgetting recent events, important
   dates, or regularly asking the same question without recalling having asked it before.

2. **Difficulty planning or following familiar steps:** Challenges following a recipe that
   was previously easy, managing regular bills, or handling numbers and schedules.

3. **Difficulty with once-familiar tasks:** Taking noticeably longer or needing help with
   everyday tasks the elder has always done independently — such as forgetting the rules
   of a familiar game, or getting lost on a usual route.

4. **Uncertainty about time or place:** Occasionally losing track of the day, date, season,
   or how they arrived somewhere.

5. **Challenges with visual or spatial understanding:** Difficulty reading, judging distances,
   or distinguishing colors — which may affect balance or safe movement.

6. **Language and conversation changes:** Pausing frequently mid-sentence, struggling to
   find the right word, or repeating the same phrase or story.

7. **Misplacing items and difficulty retracing steps:** Placing things in unusual locations
   (e.g., a phone in the refrigerator) and being unable to retrace steps to find them.

8. **Changes in judgment or decision-making:** Unusual choices with money or gifts;
   less attention to personal hygiene or grooming compared to usual habits.

9. **Withdrawal from activities and social life:** Stepping back from hobbies, outings, or
   conversations that were previously enjoyed, possibly due to difficulty keeping up.

10. **Mood and personality changes:** Appearing more easily confused, anxious, or unsettled
    when daily routines are disrupted.

## A Note for Families

Noticing one of these changes on a single occasion is usually not significant.
What matters is whether a pattern of change persists over multiple days or weeks —
and whether it represents a real shift from the elder's own earlier behavior.

If several of these patterns are observed together over time, accompanying the elder
to a family medicine or community health center for a general wellbeing conversation
is a natural and low-pressure first step.
