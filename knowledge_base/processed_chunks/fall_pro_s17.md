## Vision, Lighting, and Spatial Perception

Changes in how an elder perceives their surroundings can significantly impact their safety, making lighting and visual cues a top priority. You might consider using high-wattage, non-glare light bulbs throughout the home to ensure every room is brightly and evenly lit. Deep shadows can sometimes be misinterpreted as holes or obstacles, so it is helpful to eliminate dark corners and ensure that entryways and hallways are especially well-illuminated.

Spatial perception can also be supported through the use of high-contrast colors. For example, you might consider using a brightly colored toilet seat to make it stand out against white bathroom tiles, or placing a dark rug on a light floor to clearly mark a transition between rooms. These visual markers help the elder navigate more confidently. If you observe the elder squinting or appearing hesitant to enter a room, check if there is a harsh glare from a window or a significant difference in light levels between spaces.

Maintaining consistent lighting patterns throughout the day and evening helps the elder stay oriented to their environment. It is also helpful to ensure that their glasses are clean and their vision is checked regularly. By focusing on these environmental adjustments to support vision and spatial perception, you help the elder move through their home with a clearer understanding of their surroundings and a much lower risk of environmental confusion or accidents.
