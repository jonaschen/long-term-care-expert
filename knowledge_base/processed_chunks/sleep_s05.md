## Daytime Habits for Better Nighttime Rest

What an elder does during the daylight hours significantly influences how well they sleep at night. Physical activity is one of the best ways to promote healthy sleep. Engaging in regular, light exercise—such as a daily walk in a park or participating in a community stretching group—helps build a natural "sleep debt" that makes it easier to fall asleep in the evening. Sunlight exposure is equally important; spending time outdoors during the day helps regulate the body's internal clock and improves the production of sleep-related hormones.

Families can encourage these habits by scheduling outdoor social activities or short walks. However, it is helpful to time these activities for the morning or early afternoon. Intense physical exertion too close to bedtime might actually make it harder for some elders to settle down. Additionally, managing daytime naps is important. While a short "power nap" of 20 to 30 minutes in the early afternoon can be refreshing, long or late-afternoon naps can interfere with the ability to fall asleep at night. If the elder seems to be napping excessively during the day, it might be worth exploring if they are getting enough stimulation or if their nighttime rest needs adjustment. By fostering an active and sun-filled daytime routine, families provide the foundation for more consistent and restful nights.
