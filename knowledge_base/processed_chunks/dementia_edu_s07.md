## Managing Nighttime Restlessness

You may observe changes in the elder's sleep patterns, such as difficulty falling asleep or waking up frequently during the night. To support better rest, you might consider creating a calming evening environment. Reducing noise and dimming the lights an hour before bedtime can help signal to the body that it is time to wind down. Avoid serving beverages with caffeine, such as certain teas or coffee, in the late afternoon and evening, as these can interfere with sleep.

Daytime habits also impact nighttime rest. Encouraging the elder to be active during the day and ensuring they get some exposure to natural sunlight can help regulate their internal clock. If the elder takes long naps during the day, try to limit them to a short rest in the early afternoon so they are tired enough to sleep at night. Check that the bedroom is comfortable—not too hot or too cold—and that the bedding is familiar and cozy. If the elder wakes up confused in the middle of the night, a soft nightlight can help them orient themselves to their surroundings. If these patterns continue or become disruptive, you might consider keeping a log of their sleep and activity levels to discuss with a professional. Focusing on comfort and relaxation rather than 'forcing' sleep often leads to better outcomes for both the elder and the caregiver.
