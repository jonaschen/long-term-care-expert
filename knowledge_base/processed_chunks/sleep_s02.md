## The Importance of Sleep Quality and Deep Sleep

When observing an elder’s rest, the quality of sleep is often more important than the total number of hours. Sleep occurs in cycles, and one of the most critical stages is deep sleep, also known as slow-wave sleep. This stage is believed to be vital for physical restoration, memory support, and maintaining a healthy immune system. Achieving enough deep sleep is what helps an elder feel refreshed and mentally sharp the next day.

As people age, the amount of time spent in this deep sleep stage naturally declines. This can make sleep feel less "sturdy," meaning the elder might be more easily disturbed by noise, light, or temperature changes. Families should look for signs of daytime fatigue or irritability, which might suggest that the elder is not reaching these deeper stages of rest. It is also worth noting that deep sleep is most likely to occur earlier in the night. Therefore, maintaining a consistent bedtime that aligns with the elder’s natural tiredness can help maximize the opportunity for restorative rest. Encouraging a environment that minimizes interruptions can further support the elder in staying in these deeper stages longer, promoting better overall health and daytime vitality.
