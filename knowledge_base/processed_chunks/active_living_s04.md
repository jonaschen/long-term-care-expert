## Recognizing When to Stop and Warning Signs

While regular movement is beneficial, it is crucial to observe the elder for any signs of overexertion. Safety should always come first. The elder should stop immediately if they experience dizziness, sudden headaches, or blurred vision. Other warning signs include chest tightness, heart palpitations, or shortness of breath that makes it difficult to speak.

Compared to the usual pattern, if the elder appears unusually pale, starts sweating excessively in a cold way, or shows signs of trembling, they should sit down and rest. If discomfort in joints or muscles lasts for more than one or two hours after a session, it may indicate that the activity was too intense. If these patterns continue or if the elder does not feel better after resting, consult a professional. Monitoring these behavioral cues ensures that the activity remains a positive part of their health journey.
