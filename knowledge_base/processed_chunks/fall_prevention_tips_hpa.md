Based on the Taiwan Health Promotion Administration (HPA) elder fall prevention guidelines, here is a practical, family-friendly guide for maintaining a safe home environment and supporting the elder’s daily mobility.

## Home Environment Modifications
*   **Adequate Lighting:** Ensure all pathways, especially those leading to the bathroom, are well-lit. Consider installing motion-sensor nightlights to assist the elder during nighttime movement.
*   **Bathroom Safety:** Install sturdy grab bars near the toilet and inside the shower area. Use non-slip mats both inside and outside the bathtub or shower to prevent slipping on wet surfaces.
*   **Floor Management:** Remove loose rugs, electrical cords, and other clutter from walking paths. For necessary rugs, use double-sided tape or non-slip backing to secure them firmly to the floor.
*   **Visual Assistance:** Apply high-contrast anti-slip strips to the edges of all stairs. This helps the elder clearly distinguish each step, especially in lower light conditions.

## Exercise and Daily Habits
*   **Movement Integration:** Encourage the elder to stay active through daily tasks. For example, placing a television remote on a higher shelf can promote gentle stretching and reaching throughout the day.
*   **Strength and Balance:** Practice simple movements such as rising onto tiptoes while waiting or performing gentle half-squats while holding onto a sturdy chair. These activities help maintain lower limb strength and core stability.
*   **Consistency:** Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate activity each week, which can be broken down into manageable 10-minute sessions. Activities like Tai Chi or standing on one leg (with support) are excellent for balance.
*   **Hydration and Rest:** Ensure the elder drinks enough water throughout the day. If they have been sitting for a long time, encourage them to stand and move briefly every hour to avoid stiffness.

## Footwear and Clothing Guidance
*   **Proper Shoe Choice:** Select well-fitting, flat-soled shoes with non-slip bottoms. Shoes with velcro or laces are preferred over slip-ons to ensure they stay securely on the feet.
*   **Avoid Slippers:** While comfortable, loose slippers or going barefoot can increase the risk of a fall. Sturdy indoor shoes provide much better support and traction.
*   **Clothing Fit:** Choose trousers that are the correct length to avoid tripping. Avoid long, flowing garments or excessively loose clothing that might catch on furniture or door handles.

## Assisting with Movement and Rising
*   **The "Sit and Wait" Method:** When the elder is waking up or has been lying down, encourage them to sit at the bedside for a few moments before attempting to stand. This helps the body adjust and reduces the chance of temporary dizziness.
*   **Gentle Support:** If the elder seems to have more difficulty moving than usual, observe if this behavioral pattern persists over several days. Offering a steady arm or suggesting the use of a cane can provide extra confidence.
*   **Simplified Instructions:** When guiding the elder to move, use short, clear sentences. Give one instruction at a time and allow them plenty of time—at least 20 seconds—to process and respond to your suggestion.
*   **Professional Accompaniment:** If you observe a significant change in how the elder moves compared to their usual pattern, you might consider accompanying them to a community health center for a general wellbeing check.
