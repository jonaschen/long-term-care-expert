## Weekly Activity Targets for 65+

For elders aged 65 and above, the Taiwan Health Promotion Administration suggests a balanced approach to movement that covers three main areas: aerobic activity, muscle strengthening, and balance training. The goal is to accumulate at least 150 to 300 minutes of moderate-intensity activity throughout the week. This might sound like a lot, but it can be broken down into small, manageable sessions of 10 to 30 minutes. Every minute of movement counts toward this goal, and it is more important to be consistent than to be intense.

In addition to aerobic movement, it is recommended that the elder engages in muscle-strengthening activities at least two days a week. These don't require heavy equipment; using one's own body weight or light household items can be very effective. Strengthening activities help the elder maintain the power needed for tasks like rising from a chair or carrying groceries. Equally important is balance training, which should ideally happen three or more days per week. Activities that challenge stability, such as standing on one leg while holding onto a sturdy counter, are excellent for this purpose.

Families should observe the elder's current activity level and help them reach these targets gradually. If the elder is currently inactive, starting with just 5 or 10 minutes a day is a great first step. Compared to the usual pattern, any increase in movement is a positive change. The focus should be on a "multi-component" routine—meaning a mix of different types of movement—which provides the best support for overall physical function. If the elder has difficulty reaching these targets due to persistent changes in their movement pattern, you might consider consulting a professional for a wellbeing review, but always focus on what the elder can do rather than what they cannot.
