# Weekly Report Tone Templates

Reference for the Weekly Summary Composer skill.
Provides tone patterns, opening/closing templates, and scenario-specific
language for composing warm, family-facing weekly LINE reports.

---

## Core Tone Principles

The weekly report is the **primary relationship-building touchpoint** with
families. It must:

1. **Lead with what went well** — even in a difficult week, find and name the
   stability before naming the change.
2. **Celebrate uneventfulness** — a quiet week is good news; make it feel that way.
3. **Frame changes as gentle observations** — what the sensor noticed, not what
   it means.
4. **Offer one or two doable ideas** — concrete, low-effort, non-medical.
5. **Close with a question** — invite the family into dialogue about their elder's
   daily life.

## Opening Templates by Week Type

### Quiet Week (No Anomalies)

> "This week, [elder's/home's] daily rhythms looked a lot like what we've come
> to know — reassuring consistency in the routine. Here's a brief look at the
> week."

> "A steady, settled week — and that's a good thing. When the daily patterns
> hold close to what's been typical, it means the routines that support
> comfortable living are doing their work."

> "Not much to flag this week — and that's the best kind of update. The week
> looked comfortable and consistent from the sensor's perspective."

---

### Week with One Mild Observation

> "This week was mostly settled — with one small thing worth a gentle mention."

> "Overall, a comfortable week. There was one pattern that was a little
> different from the usual — not a concern, just something worth keeping
> a gentle eye on."

> "The week looked well within the familiar range, with one quiet observation
> to share."

---

### Week with Multiple Observations (Complex)

> "This week had a few things worth sharing — nothing urgent, but a fuller
> picture than a typical week."

> "The past week brought a few gentle observations across different parts of
> the daily routine. Here's what we noticed."

> "A week with a bit more to share than usual — several small patterns that,
> taken together, give a fuller picture of the week."

---

### Week Following an Alert Week

> "Following last week's note about [brief reference], this week's patterns
> are worth looking at together."

> "After the observation we shared last week, it's helpful to see how this
> week compared. Here's the picture."

## Section Templates

### Sleep Section

**Stable sleep:**
"Sleep patterns this week looked comfortable — the nights were settled and
within the familiar range."

**Mild disruption:**
"The nights were a little busier than the usual pattern — there were some brief
moments of nighttime activity over a few nights. This is common and often
resolves on its own."

**Continued pattern:**
"The nighttime pattern we mentioned last week continued into this week —
still calm in tone, but worth noting that it's persisted a bit longer than usual."

---

### Mobility Section

**Stable mobility:**
"Movement through the home looked comfortable this week — a steady, familiar
pace and rhythm."

**Mild gait change:**
"The sensor noticed that movement was a little slower than usual this week —
a gentle dip from the typical pace. This often reflects a tired day or change
in routine."

**Rising difficulty:**
"There were a few moments where getting up from a seated position seemed to
take a bit more effort than usual. This is worth watching gently over the
coming week."

---

### Cognitive/Daily Routine Section

**Stable routine:**
"The daily rhythm looked familiar and consistent this week — good signs that
the routine is comfortable."

**Quiet/reduced activity:**
"The week was a little quieter than usual in terms of movement around the
home — fewer trips between rooms than the typical pattern. Sometimes a quiet
week is just a quiet week."

**Appliance observation:**
"There was one extended kitchen moment this week that was a little longer than
usual — worth a gentle check-in, but no cause for concern on its own."

---

### Lifestyle/Activity Section

**On target:**
"Activity levels this week were close to the usual pattern — steady movement
throughout the days."

**Slightly reduced:**
"This week's overall movement was a little lower than the week before — a gentle
dip that sometimes just reflects a cooler week or a change in energy."

**Recovering:**
"After last week's quieter activity, this week showed a bit more movement —
a reassuring shift back toward the usual pattern."

## Closing Questions (Engagement Openers)

The weekly report always closes with a warm question that invites dialogue.
Rotate these to avoid repetition:

**Routine-focused:**
- "Is there anything about the week that stood out to you that the sensor
  might not have captured?"
- "Did anything happen this week that we might have missed?"

**Activity-focused:**
- "What was your elder doing this week that felt most comfortable and familiar?"
- "Were there any enjoyable moments this week — a walk, a meal together,
  something that felt particularly nice?"

**Environment-focused:**
- "Is there anything about the home setup that's been on your mind lately?"
- "Are there any small changes you've been wondering about making to help
  the daily routine feel a bit smoother?"

**Relationship-focused:**
- "What's one thing about the elder's week that we can't see in the data —
  but you noticed?"
- "How has the elder seemed to you this week, in your own words?"

## Tone Calibration by Situation

### When Delivering a No-Change Week

Avoid: "Nothing to report." (flat, cold)
Use: "A steady, comfortable week — the patterns we've come to know." (warm, validating)

### When Noting a Persistent Pattern

Avoid: "This problem has continued for two weeks." (clinical, alarming)
Use: "The pattern we noticed last week was still present this week — gentle,
but worth keeping in view as we move into the next week." (observational, continuous)

### When Suggesting Professional Consultation

Avoid: "You should see a doctor." (directive, alarming)
Use: "If this continues into the next week or two, it might be worth describing
what you've noticed at the next regular checkup — just so the doctor has the
full picture." (gentle, non-urgent)

## Structural Template (Full Report)

```
[Warm opening — week type]

[Sleep section — 2-3 sentences]

[Mobility section — 2-3 sentences]

[Daily rhythm/cognitive section — 2-3 sentences, only if observations exist]

[One suggestion — concrete, HPA-sourced, low-effort]

[Closing question — invites dialogue]

[Auto-injected legal disclaimer]
```

**Total target length:** 150–250 words. The weekly report is a warm,
readable message — not a comprehensive report. Families read it on their
phone in under a minute.

---

## What to Omit

- Never include raw numbers, timestamps, or sensor identifiers
- Never include medical terms or condition names
- Never list every observation from every skill — synthesize and select
- Never use bullet points in the family-facing message — flowing prose only
- Never include both a suggestion AND a consultation recommendation in the
  same report — choose one based on the situation
