# HPA Sleep Hygiene Reference

Key guidance from Taiwan Health Promotion Administration sleep hygiene handbooks.
Used as internal context for the Sleep Pattern Expert skill.
All content is lifestyle and environmental — no pharmaceutical recommendations.

---

## Sleep Changes in Later Life (What Is Normal)

Elders commonly experience:
- Earlier sleep onset and earlier waking (circadian shift — normal, not a problem)
- More frequent brief awakenings during the night
- Lighter sleep overall — deeper sleep stages become shorter
- More sensitivity to light, noise, and temperature changes at night
- Natural reduction in total sleep need (7–8 hours vs. 8–9 in younger adults)

**Framing for families:** These changes are part of healthy aging. The goal
is not to restore a younger sleep pattern, but to support comfortable, safe
rest within the elder's current natural rhythm.

---

## Environment: Bedroom Setup

- **Temperature:** Keep the bedroom cool but not cold — elders are more
  sensitive to temperature extremes. A light blanket for warmth is better
  than a heavy, restrictive one.
- **Light:** Blackout curtains or an eye mask help if early morning light
  causes early waking. A very dim nightlight (red or amber, not blue/white)
  is safer than complete darkness for nighttime bathroom trips.
- **Noise:** Consistent low background sound (fan, soft nature sounds) can
  mask sudden noises that trigger brief awakenings.
- **Bed comfort:** The mattress and pillow should support comfortable
  positions. If the elder is frequently adjusting position, consider
  whether the surface is appropriate.

---

## Daytime Habits That Support Night Sleep

- **Morning sunlight:** 20–30 minutes of natural light exposure in the
  morning anchors the circadian rhythm and helps consolidate night sleep.
- **Physical activity:** Regular gentle movement (walking, stretching)
  during the day deepens sleep quality at night. Avoid vigorous activity
  within 2–3 hours of bedtime.
- **Naps:** Short naps (20–30 minutes) before 15:00 are generally fine.
  Long afternoon naps (> 1 hour) or naps after 15:00 often delay nighttime
  sleep onset.
- **Caffeine:** Avoid coffee, strong tea, and caffeinated beverages after
  14:00. Effects last longer in older adults.

---

## Evening Routine

- **Consistent schedule:** Going to bed and waking at the same time daily —
  including weekends — is the most effective single behavioral support for
  sleep quality.
- **Wind-down period:** 30–60 minutes of quiet activity before bed (reading,
  gentle stretching, soft music, warm bathing) signals to the body that
  sleep is approaching.
- **Fluid management:** Reduce fluid intake after 19:00 to decrease
  nighttime bathroom trips. Ensure the elder has had adequate hydration
  earlier in the day.
- **Light and screens:** Bright overhead lighting and screens (phones, TV)
  suppress sleep-onset signals. Dim lights and gentle activity in the
  hour before bed.
- **Heavy meals:** Avoid large meals within 2 hours of bedtime. A light
  snack is acceptable if the elder feels hungry.

---

## Nighttime Safety (Bathroom Trips)

Nighttime bathroom trips are common in elders and are a leading fall risk.

- **Pathway lighting:** Install motion-activated nightlights from bedroom
  to bathroom. Light level should be enough to see clearly, not bright
  enough to fully wake the elder.
- **Clear path:** Remove rugs, cables, and obstacles from the nighttime path.
  Non-slip flooring or grip tape on bathroom tiles.
- **Grab bars:** Install near the toilet and in the shower/tub area.
- **Bedside commode:** For elders with very frequent nighttime needs,
  a bedside commode reduces the distance traveled.
- **Footwear:** Non-slip slippers should be within easy reach of the bed.
  Bare feet on smooth floors at night is a fall risk.

## When to Gently Suggest a Professional Conversation

Use this language — never suggest diagnoses or treatments:

- "If this pattern continues for more than two weeks, it might be worth
  mentioning at the next regular checkup."
- "The family can describe what they've noticed and ask whether there's
  anything simple worth trying."
- "A conversation with a professional isn't about finding something wrong —
  it's about getting tailored ideas for a better routine."
