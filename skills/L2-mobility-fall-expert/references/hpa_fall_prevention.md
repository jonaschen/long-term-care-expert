# HPA Fall Prevention Reference

Key guidance from Taiwan Health Promotion Administration fall prevention
handbooks (長者防跌妙招手冊, 老人防跌工作手冊).
Used as internal context for the Mobility and Fall Risk Expert skill.

---

## Understanding Fall Risk in Elders (Observational Framework)

Falls are the leading cause of injury in older adults. Sensor-detectable
precursors include:

- **Gait slowdown:** Walking pace 30%+ below personal baseline is a
  meaningful signal. Gradual pace reduction over days is more significant
  than a single slow reading.
- **Difficulty rising:** Repeated failed rise attempts from chairs or bed
  indicate reduced lower limb strength and/or balance difficulty.
- **Shuffling:** Short step length and reduced foot clearance increase
  tripping risk — often appears alongside overall gait slowdown.
- **Sudden posture drop:** Rapid, large height decrease detected by sensor
  — requires immediate family notification. Cannot confirm a fall has
  occurred; family must verify.

**Important:** Never classify an elder as "a fall risk" or "high risk for
falls." Describe what the sensor observed. The family and their
professional contacts assess risk.

## Environmental Modifications (Priority Order)

### Bathroom (Highest Risk Location)
- Grab bars beside toilet and in shower/tub — installed into wall studs
- Non-slip mat on bathroom floor (suction-cup base)
- Non-slip strips or coating in shower/bathtub floor
- Raised toilet seat if the elder struggles to lower/rise from seated position
- Nightlight inside bathroom

### Bedroom
- Bed height adjusted so feet rest flat on floor when sitting on edge
- Non-slip mat at bedside (where first step lands)
- Clear path from bed to door and to bathroom
- Items the elder frequently reaches for placed at waist height

### Living Areas
- Remove loose rugs or secure with non-slip backing and double-sided tape
- Clear main walking paths of cables, furniture corners, and clutter
- Ensure adequate lighting — shadows and dark corners are hazards
- Furniture arranged to allow the elder to touch a surface for stability
  while moving through the room

### Stairs (If Applicable)
- Handrail on both sides if possible; at minimum one side
- Non-slip strips on each step edge
- Ensure adequate lighting top and bottom
- Encourage use of the handrail on every trip

### Footwear
- Non-slip soles with good heel support
- Properly fitted — neither too tight nor loose enough to slip off
- Avoid slippers that slide, flip-flops, or walking in socks on smooth floors

## Gentle Exercise for Fall Prevention (Lifestyle Suggestions)

Based on HPA recommendations — always framed as enjoyable activity, not
"exercise for safety":

- **Balance standing:** Hold a chair back and practice standing on one foot
  briefly — even 10 seconds per side, daily
- **Leg raises:** Seated or standing, lift each leg slowly and hold
- **Heel-to-toe walking:** Walk slowly in a straight line, placing heel
  directly in front of the other foot's toes
- **Sit-to-stand:** Rise from a chair slowly without using hands if possible;
  build strength gradually
- **Walking:** Regular walking — even short daily distances — maintains
  the gait pattern and lower limb strength

**Framing:** "Some families find that a short daily walk together makes the
day feel a bit brighter — and it's one of the simplest things that supports
comfortable movement."

## Rising Safely from Chairs and Bed

A high-fall-risk moment that sensor `rise_attempt_fail` captures:

1. **From chair:** Edge forward to the front of the seat first, then lean
   forward slightly, then use hands on armrests or thighs to push up
2. **From bed:** Roll to the side first, push up sideways to sitting,
   pause for 30 seconds before standing (prevents dizziness from postural
   change)
3. **Chair selection:** Firm chair with armrests at appropriate height —
   soft, deep sofas are very difficult to rise from safely

## When to Accompany Elder for a Professional Conversation

- Gait change or rise difficulty persisting more than 7–10 days
- Any occurrence of a sudden posture drop (after verifying elder is safe)
- Elder expressing fear of falling, or changing behavior to avoid movement

Language: "If this continues, it might be worth describing what you've
noticed at the next regular checkup — just so it's on the record."
