# Healthy Lifestyle Reference — HPA Guidelines

Reference for the Chronic Disease Lifestyle Observer skill.
Based on Taiwan Health Promotion Administration activity and lifestyle guidelines.
For internal use — informs observational language about activity trends and
daily routine regularity. All content is lifestyle and environmental only —
no medical claims, no condition-specific content.

---

## Purpose of This Reference

This skill observes **lifestyle regularity patterns** — not health conditions.
The guidelines below inform what consistent, health-supporting daily habits
look like for older adults, so the skill can describe deviations from those
patterns in warm, actionable language.

The word "chronic disease" in the skill name refers to the *HPA guideline
category* that addresses lifestyle factors known to support long-term wellbeing
in older adults. The skill never names, implies, or infers any health condition.

---

## HPA Activity Guidelines for Older Adults

### Weekly Activity Targets

Taiwan HPA physical activity guidelines for adults 65+:

| Activity Type | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Moderate aerobic activity | ≥ 150 minutes/week | Walking, tai chi, cycling |
| Vigorous aerobic activity | ≥ 75 minutes/week | Alternative to moderate |
| Muscle-strengthening activity | ≥ 2 days/week | Resistance, balance, flexibility |
| Reduce sedentary time | Break up sitting every 60–90 min | Any brief movement counts |

**Important:** These are *context* for understanding activity trends, not targets
to cite in family messages. Translate into warm observational language only.

---

### Activity Patterns That Support Daily Wellbeing

Based on HPA lifestyle guidelines:

**Morning:**
- Natural light exposure within 1 hour of waking (supports circadian anchoring)
- Gentle movement or stretching to start the day
- Consistent breakfast timing

**Daytime:**
- Regular gentle movement — even short walks around the home or courtyard
- Engagement with familiar activities (gardening, household tasks, social calls)
- Avoiding prolonged sitting stretches (> 60–90 minutes without moving)

**Evening:**
- Winding down activity level gradually after 18:00
- Consistent dinner timing
- Gentle, low-stimulation activities before bed

## Data Translation Rules

Transform activity data into warm lifestyle language:

| Sensor Pattern | Warm Language |
|---|---|
| Activity volume -20% vs prior week | "This week has been a little quieter than the week before — a gentle, gradual shift." |
| Activity volume -30% vs prior week | "There's been noticeably less movement this week than what we usually see." |
| Irregular daily timing (meals, activity) | "The daily rhythm has been a bit less consistent this week." |
| Regular daily timing restored | "The daily routine has been more settled this week — a reassuring sign." |
| Consistent step/movement counts | "The week's rhythm has been nicely consistent with the usual pattern." |
| Increased daytime inactivity | "The afternoons have been quieter than usual — there were some longer stretches of stillness." |

## Lifestyle Regularity: What Matters Most

HPA guidelines highlight these as the highest-impact lifestyle factors for
supporting long-term wellbeing in older adults:

### 1. Daily Schedule Consistency

Going to bed, waking, and eating at consistent times is one of the most
powerful supports for overall wellbeing. Irregular timing disrupts sleep,
appetite, and energy levels.

**Sensor signal:** Timing variance in usual daily events (movement start,
activity peaks, quiet periods).

**Family suggestion language:**
"Keeping a gentle, consistent daily schedule — even on quieter days —
can make a big difference in how settled and comfortable the days feel."

---

### 2. Regular Gentle Movement

Brief, frequent movement throughout the day (rather than one concentrated
exercise session) is the most sustainable pattern for older adults.

**HPA framing:** Walking to the kitchen for tea, tending to plants, a short
walk after meals — these everyday movements count.

**Family suggestion language:**
"Even gentle everyday movement — a slow walk, time in the garden — helps
maintain the comfortable rhythms of daily life."

---

### 3. Social Engagement

Regular social contact — meals with family, phone calls, community activities —
supports both physical and emotional wellbeing.

**Sensor signal:** May correlate with reduced outings or fewer departure/return
events during typical social hours.

**Family suggestion language:**
"Regular connection with familiar people — even brief moments — is one of the
simplest things that makes daily life feel fuller."

---

### 4. Sunlight and Outdoor Time

Natural light exposure, especially in the morning, supports sleep quality
and general energy levels.

**Family suggestion language:**
"A gentle walk outdoors — even just to the building entrance — is one of the
simplest things that helps the body keep its natural rhythms."

---

## Observational Framing for Families

When describing lifestyle trend changes, use these patterns:

**For a declining trend:**
"This week's activity pattern was a little lower than the week before — a gentle
dip that sometimes just reflects a quieter week. If it continues for another
week or two, it might be worth a gentle check-in."

**For recovery toward baseline:**
"This week's rhythm looks a bit more like the usual pattern — a reassuring sign."

**For sustained change:**
"Over the past few weeks, activity has been a bit lower than the longer-term
pattern — worth keeping a gentle eye on, and perhaps mentioning at the next
routine checkup."

---

## When to Suggest a Professional Conversation

Use this language — never suggest diagnoses or interpretations:

"If the quieter pattern continues for more than two to three weeks, it might
be worth mentioning to the elder's regular doctor — just to share what you've
noticed. A brief description of the change in daily routine is enough."

"You don't need to know what it means — describing what you observe is the
most useful thing to share."
