# Behavioral Change Observation Guide

Reference for the Dementia Behavior Expert skill.
Guides warm, observational language when describing changes in daily functioning.
**Critical constraint:** This guide is for internal reasoning only. Never expose
clinical frameworks, assessment names, or numbered criteria in family output.
All items must be translated into daily-life observations only.

---

## Why Daily Functioning Changes Matter

Changes in everyday routines — how someone manages familiar tasks, navigates
their home at night, or engages with activities they used to enjoy — can be
meaningful signals worth gently noting. These changes are common as people age
and should be described with curiosity and warmth, never with alarm or labels.

The sensor observes *what is happening in the home.* The family — together with
professionals they trust — interprets what it means.

---

## Observable Patterns and Warm Language Translations

### Memory for Recent Events

**What the sensor might detect:** Repeated kitchen interactions around the same
task; returning to appliances multiple times in a short period.

**Warm language:**
- "The sensor noticed a few extra visits to the kitchen this morning — sometimes
  this just means a busy morning routine."
- "There were a couple of return visits to the kitchen area — worth keeping
  a gentle eye on whether this becomes a pattern."

**Never say:** "memory loss detected," "forgetfulness," "cognitive symptoms"

---

### Interest and Engagement in Daily Activities

**What the sensor might detect:** Prolonged daytime inactivity — the elder
remains in one area for an extended period without the usual movement between
rooms.

**Warm language:**
- "Compared to the usual daily rhythm, there was a quieter stretch in the
  afternoon — less movement around the home than we typically see."
- "The past couple of days have been a little quieter than usual — the elder
  spent more time in one area without the usual gentle movement through the home."

**Never say:** "loss of interest," "apathy," "behavioral symptom"

---

### Managing Familiar Household Tasks

**What the sensor might detect:** Unusually long appliance interaction —
time spent with a familiar appliance (stove, kettle) significantly exceeds
the elder's personal baseline.

**Warm language:**
- "The sensor noticed the elder spent a bit longer with the stove area than
  usual — it may just be a different recipe, but worth a gentle check-in."
- "There was an extended period of interaction in the kitchen that was a bit
  different from the usual pattern."

**Never say:** "difficulty using appliances," "task completion problems,"
"functional decline"

---

### Orientation to Time and Environment

**What the sensor might detect:** Nighttime movement outside the usual
bathroom-trip pattern — movement during sleeping hours in areas that aren't
on the usual bedroom-to-bathroom path.

**Warm language:**
- "Over the past few nights, the sensor noticed some movement during the
  overnight hours — a little more than the usual brief bathroom trip."
- "There were a few overnight moments of activity that weren't the usual
  brief bathroom visit — it might be worth checking whether the elder is
  sleeping comfortably."

**Never say:** "nighttime wandering," "disorientation," "confusion"

---

### Handling Finances and Complex Planning

**Not directly sensor-detectable.** If families raise concerns about this
domain, gently suggest describing the observation at the next routine checkup
without offering interpretation.

---

### Judgment in Social Situations

**Not directly sensor-detectable.** Treat as family-raised concern only.

---

### Community Independence (Outings, Transport)

**What the sensor might detect:** Reduced departure/return events compared to
the elder's baseline — fewer exit/entry events during typical outing hours.

**Warm language:**
- "The past week or so has been a little quieter in terms of outings —
  fewer departures from home than the usual pattern."
- "The elder's daily movements have been more home-centered lately than
  what we usually see."

**Never say:** "reduced community participation," "social withdrawal," "IADL decline"

---

## Internal Calibration Notes (Never Expose to Families)

The following calibration signals are for internal reasoning only — they help
determine whether an observed pattern warrants escalated attention. Never
reference these categories in family-facing output.

| Internal signal | Escalation consideration |
|---|---|
| Inactivity ≥ 4 hours on multiple days | Higher weight than single-day event |
| Appliance anomaly during nighttime hours | Elevated concern vs. daytime |
| Nighttime movement outside bathroom path | Immediate attention consideration |
| Multi-domain convergence (inactivity + appliance + nighttime movement) | Strongest signal combination |

---

## Escalation Language

When a pattern persists or involves multiple signals:

"If this pattern continues, it might be worth mentioning what you've noticed
at the next regular checkup — just so your doctor has the full picture."

"Families sometimes find it helpful to keep a simple log of what they notice
over a week or two, so they have something concrete to share at their next
regular visit."

**Never suggest a specific condition is the cause.** The observation is the
message — interpretation belongs to professionals.

---

## Tone Reminders

- These observations are **common** in aging — normalize before suggesting action.
- Lead with warmth: "things can shift a little as routines evolve..."
- End with agency: "you know your elder best — trust what you notice."
- Never express urgency in the language itself, even for elevated-concern patterns.
  Urgency is expressed through routing (L1 escalation), not through family-facing words.
