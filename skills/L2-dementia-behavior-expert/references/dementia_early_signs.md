# Early Behavioral Change Signs — HPA Reference

Reference for the Dementia Behavior Expert skill.
Based on Taiwan Health Promotion Administration caregiver guidance.
For internal use — informs warm observational language for families.
All content is environmental and observational — no medical advice.

---

## HPA Framework: Early Observable Signs in Daily Life

The HPA caregiver handbooks describe early behavioral changes in terms of
*shifts in daily routines and functioning* — not clinical criteria. This
is the framework that guides this skill's language.

---

## What Families Commonly Notice First

### 1. Repetition in Daily Routines

Repeating the same question, task, or story within a short time. Sensor
proxy: repeated appliance interactions or kitchen returns within a brief window.

**HPA guidance:** This is common and varies day to day. Family support means
responding patiently without drawing attention to the repetition — this reduces
distress for the elder.

**Warm language for families:**
"Some days routines involve a few extra loops — this is common and doesn't
require a reaction each time."

---

### 2. Changes in Initiative for Familiar Activities

Doing less of activities that were once regular — hobbies, social calls,
daily walks. Sensor proxy: reduced movement events or longer sedentary
stretches during hours when the elder is typically active.

**HPA guidance:** Gentle encouragement to maintain familiar activities
is more effective than creating new ones. The comfort of the familiar routine
matters more than the activity itself.

**Warm language for families:**
"The past few days have been a little quieter than the elder's usual rhythm.
Familiar activities — even brief ones — can make a gentle difference."

---

### 3. Difficulty with Previously Familiar Tasks

Taking longer than usual with tasks that were once easy — kitchen routines,
managing the television remote, navigating familiar household items. Sensor
proxy: anomalously long appliance interaction duration.

**HPA guidance:** Simplifying the task environment — reducing the number of
steps required, keeping frequently used items visible and accessible — reduces
frustration without drawing attention to the difficulty.

**Environmental suggestions:**
- Keep frequently used items in consistent, visible locations
- Reduce the number of steps for common tasks (pre-set familiar settings)
- Label items with simple, large text if helpful

---

### 4. Changes in Sleep-Wake Timing

Sleeping at unusual hours, increased nighttime activity, daytime drowsiness.
Sensor proxy: bed exits during overnight hours in non-bathroom areas; changes
in daytime activity timing.

**HPA guidance:** Maintaining consistent daily schedule — regular meals, gentle
activity at consistent times, natural light exposure — helps anchor the day-night
rhythm more than any single intervention.

**Warm language for families:**
"The nighttime has been a little busier than usual — keeping a gentle, consistent
daytime routine can help the nights become a bit more settled over time."

---

### 5. Changes in Social Engagement

Withdrawing from conversations or family gatherings that were previously enjoyed.
Less initiation of interaction. Not directly sensor-detectable but may correlate
with reduced movement and longer inactivity periods.

**HPA guidance:** Engagement with familiar people and familiar topics — rather
than new experiences — is most comfortable. Simple, low-pressure connection
(shared meals, familiar music, a brief walk together) maintains the bond without
creating performance anxiety.

## Home Environment Adjustments (HPA-Based)

These environmental adjustments are drawn from HPA caregiver guidance:

### Safety-Oriented

| Area | Adjustment | Why It Helps |
|---|---|---|
| Kitchen | Automatic shut-off for stove/kettle | Reduces consequence of extended or forgotten appliance use |
| Hallways | Night lights on bedroom-to-bathroom path | Supports safe nighttime navigation |
| Entrance | Door alert or monitoring | Awareness of nighttime departures |
| Medications | Organized pill dispenser | Removes complexity from daily routine |

### Comfort-Oriented

| Area | Adjustment | Why It Helps |
|---|---|---|
| Living area | Keep familiar items visible and in consistent locations | Reduces disorientation when looking for things |
| Bedroom | Consistent layout — do not rearrange furniture | Familiar spatial memory remains intact longer than other memory types |
| Kitchen | Label frequently used items simply | Reduces confusion without drawing attention to difficulty |
| Common areas | Display familiar family photos at eye level | Provides orientation and emotional comfort |

## Caregiver Support Reminders

HPA guidance emphasizes that caregivers need support too. When appropriate:

"Caring for someone whose daily patterns are shifting takes patience — and
the family's own wellbeing matters too. Taiwan's local caregiver support
resources can be a helpful sounding board."

Language to use when a family seems overwhelmed:
"You're doing something really meaningful by paying attention to these small
changes. That awareness itself is a form of care."

---

## When to Gently Suggest a Professional Conversation

HPA guidance: frame this as information-sharing, not alarm.

- If a pattern has persisted for **2 weeks or more**
- If the family has noticed the same thing independently
- If the elder has expressed distress or fear about their own changes

Language:
"If this pattern continues, it might be worth describing what you've noticed
at the next regular checkup — just so it's on the doctor's radar. You don't
need to have all the answers; describing what you observe is enough."

**Never:** name a condition, suggest a diagnosis, or imply certainty about
what the pattern means.
